üìä Key Category Performance Metrics

Real CPG metrics used by retailers and manufacturers to evaluate category health:

üí∞ Sales Revenue

Definition: Total dollar volume sold in the category

Why it matters: Determines category importance to overall store revenue. High-sales categories get priority in shelf space allocation and promotional calendars.

üì¶ Unit Velocity

Definition: Units sold per store per week

Why it matters: THE most important CPG metric. Shows product movement rate. Velocity <1 unit/store/week = consider discontinuation. Velocity >5 = increase facings.

üéØ Market Share

Definition: % of total category sales your products capture

Why it matters: Indicates competitive position. Track share gains/losses to understand if growth is from category expansion or stealing share from competitors.

üè∑Ô∏è Promotional Lift

Definition: % sales increase during promotion vs baseline

Why it matters: Measures promo efficiency. Lift <20% = weak promo. 20-50% = good. >50% = strong response. Must exceed cost of discount + display.

üíµ Gross Margin %

Definition: (Sales - COGS) / Sales

Why it matters: Profitability metric. Must balance with velocity‚Äîhigh margin but slow velocity ties up shelf space. Target: 20-30% for branded, 30-40% for private label.

üìà YoY Growth Rate

Definition: % change in sales vs same period last year

Why it matters: Shows category momentum. Compare dollar growth vs unit growth to understand if growth is from volume (good) or just price increases. Track both for complete picture.

üè™ Private Label Share

Definition: % of category sales from store brands

Why it matters: Private label drives margin expansion (typically 300-500bps higher than national brands). Target: 25-35% for mature categories. Too low = margin opportunity. Too high = risk brand erosion.

üë• Panel Metrics: Understanding Shopper Behavior

Panel metrics (from household panel data like Nielsen, IRI, or Circana) break down category performance by shopper behavior, not just sales. This reveals WHY a category is growing or declining.

üè† Household Penetration

Definition: % of all households that bought from the category in the past year

Example: 78% HH penetration for Beverages = 78 out of 100 households bought beverages

Insight: High penetration (70%+) = mass market category. Low penetration (20-40%) = niche. Growing penetration means attracting new buyers.

üõí Buying Rate

Definition: Average units purchased per buying household per year

Example: 12.4 buying rate = each household that buys beverages purchases 12.4 units/year

Insight: Increasing buying rate means existing customers are consuming more. Focus on usage occasions and basket building to drive this up.

üîÑ Purchase Frequency

Definition: Average number of shopping trips with category purchase per year

Example: 8.2 frequency = buyers purchase beverages on 8.2 different shopping trips/year

Insight: High frequency (8+) = routine replenishment. Low (2-4) = considered purchase. Promotions drive frequency up for price-sensitive categories.

üìä The Growth Equation

Category Sales = Household Penetration √ó Buying Rate √ó Purchase Frequency √ó Price per Unit

This decomposition helps diagnose growth: Are we attracting new buyers (penetration), getting buyers to buy more (buying rate), increasing trip frequency, or raising prices? Each lever requires different tactics.

üéØ Category Role Strategy Framework

The industry-standard approach to categorizing products by strategic role (not the BCG matrix):

üéØ

Destination Categories

Definition: Categories shoppers make special trips for. Must-win categories that drive store choice.

Examples: Fresh produce, meat/seafood, pharmacy (drugstores), diapers (for parents)

Strategy: Maximize selection, competitive pricing, high in-stock, prominent placement. Willing to sacrifice margin for traffic.

üîÑ

Routine Categories

Definition: Regular, frequent purchases. Basket builders that drive frequency.

Examples: Beverages, snacks, bread, milk, eggs, household cleaners

Strategy: Balance assortment with space efficiency. Optimize for velocity and margin. Use promotions to drive traffic during slow periods.

üéÑ

Seasonal/Occasional

Definition: Holiday or event-driven purchases with predictable spikes.

Examples: Halloween candy, BBQ supplies (summer), cold/flu (winter), school supplies

Strategy: Heavy promotion during season, minimal off-season presence. Maximize margin during peak. Use end-caps and displays.

‚ö°

Convenience/Fill-in

Definition: Low-frequency impulse or emergency purchases. Not planned.

Examples: Batteries, greeting cards, first aid, travel-size items, checkout candy

Strategy: Premium pricing (low elasticity), minimal facings, strategic placement (checkout, end-caps). Focus on high-margin items.

üéØ The 4Ps of Category Management

Tactical levers category managers use to optimize performance:

üì¶ Product (Assortment)

What it is: SKU selection and variety depth

  • ‚Ä¢ SKU rationalization: Cut SKUs with velocity <1 unit/store/week
  • ‚Ä¢ Add premium/natural variants in growing segments
  • ‚Ä¢ Private label penetration targets (25-35% of category)
  • ‚Ä¢ Manage brand portfolio: national brands, regional, private label

üí∞ Price

What it is: Everyday pricing and promotional pricing strategy

  • ‚Ä¢ Price elasticity analysis: test 5-10% price changes
  • ‚Ä¢ Competitive price positioning vs market basket
  • ‚Ä¢ Hi-Lo vs EDLP (Everyday Low Price) strategy by category
  • ‚Ä¢ Promotional calendar: depth of discount, frequency, timing

üìç Place (Merchandising)

What it is: Shelf placement and space allocation

  • ‚Ä¢ Planogram optimization: allocate facings based on velocity √ó margin
  • ‚Ä¢ Eye-level = buy level: premium placement for high-margin SKUs
  • ‚Ä¢ Cross-merchandising: place complementary items together
  • ‚Ä¢ Secondary placements: end-caps, displays, checkout

üéÅ Promotion

What it is: Advertising, features, displays, TPRs

  • ‚Ä¢ TPR (Temporary Price Reduction): BOGO, $1 off, % discounts
  • ‚Ä¢ Feature ads: weekly circular, digital coupons
  • ‚Ä¢ Display effectiveness: end-cap lift 200-400%
  • ‚Ä¢ Measure incremental volume vs baseline to calculate ROI
