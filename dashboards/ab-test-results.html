üî¨ The Science Behind A/B Testing

A/B testing is the gold standard for causal inference in product decisions. Here's what we're looking for in every experiment:

üìä Statistical Significance (p-value)

What it is: The probability that the observed difference occurred by chance.

Our threshold: p < 0.05 (95% confidence)

Why it matters: Ensures we don't ship changes that aren't actually better, avoiding wasted engineering effort and potential user harm.

üìà Effect Size (Lift %)

What it is: The magnitude of improvement from control to variant.

Our threshold: Minimum 2% lift for high-effort changes

Why it matters: Even statistically significant results need meaningful business impact to justify implementation costs.

üë• Sample Size & Power

What it is: Number of users needed to detect true effects reliably.

Our approach: Pre-calculate using power analysis (80% power minimum)

Why it matters: Prevents inconclusive tests and ensures we can detect meaningful differences when they exist.

‚ö†Ô∏è Type I & II Errors

Type I (False Positive): Shipping a change that doesn't actually work (Œ± = 5%)

Type II (False Negative): Missing a real improvement (Œ≤ = 20%)

Why it matters: Balances innovation risk with scientific rigor‚Äîwe prefer missing winners to shipping losers.

‚öôÔ∏è Our Testing Methodology

Every experiment follows a rigorous 5-stage process to ensure validity and actionable results:

1

Hypothesis Formation

Define the problem, identify the metric to optimize, and state a falsifiable hypothesis. Example: "Changing the CTA button from 'Learn More' to 'Start Free Trial' will increase click-through rate by at least 10% because it's more action-oriented."

2

Experiment Design

Calculate required sample size, define success metrics (primary & secondary), set significance threshold (Œ± = 0.05), and establish minimum detectable effect (MDE). Determine test duration based on traffic.

3

Randomization & Execution

Implement proper randomization (50/50 split typical), ensure consistent user assignment, validate tracking instrumentation, and monitor for implementation bugs or sample ratio mismatch (SRM).

4

Statistical Analysis

Calculate p-value using appropriate test (z-test for proportions, t-test for continuous metrics), measure effect size, compute confidence intervals, and check for novelty effects or time-based patterns.

5

Decision & Rollout

Ship: p < 0.05 AND lift meets business threshold | Iterate: Directionally positive but underpowered | Kill: Negative impact or no difference | Extend: Trending positive but needs more data

üìã Testing Best Practices & Guardrails

Rules we follow to maintain scientific rigor and avoid common pitfalls

‚úÖ Do's

  • ‚Ä¢ Pre-register hypothesis and success metrics
  • ‚Ä¢ Run power analysis to determine sample size
  • ‚Ä¢ Randomize at user level, not session level
  • ‚Ä¢ Wait for pre-calculated sample size before peeking
  • ‚Ä¢ Check for novelty effects (run 1-2 full weeks minimum)
  • ‚Ä¢ Track secondary metrics for unintended consequences
  • ‚Ä¢ Document learnings even from "failed" tests

‚ùå Don'ts

  • ‚Ä¢ Don't peek at results before reaching sample size
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't change hypothesis mid-test
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't run multiple tests on same traffic simultaneously
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't ship tests that "almost" reached significance
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't ignore Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM) warnings
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't test without clear success criteria
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't assume correlation = causation without control group
