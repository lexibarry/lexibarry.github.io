üéØ What is Go-To-Market (GTM) Readiness?

GTM readiness is the systematic process of preparing all cross-functional teams and assets before launching a product, feature, or initiative. It ensures that Product, Marketing, Sales, Customer Success, Legal, and Operations are aligned, resourced, and ready to execute on launch day.

Why it matters: According to Harvard Business Review, 75% of product launches fail to meet their goals, often due to poor cross-functional coordination, not product quality. A rigorous GTM process reduces launch risk, accelerates time-to-value, and ensures all teams can support customers from day one.

‚ö†Ô∏è Cost of Failed Launches: Missed revenue targets, confused customers, overwhelmed support teams, brand damage, and wasted engineering effort. GTM readiness prevents these expensive failures through systematic preparation and risk mitigation.

üìä Launch Tier Framework

Not all launches are equal. Tier your launches by impact to allocate appropriate resources and rigor:

üî• Tier 1: Major Launch

Impact: Company-defining. New product lines, major features that change positioning.

Examples: New paid tier, API platform launch, entering new market

GTM Requirements: Full cross-functional readiness, exec review, PR/AR strategy, sales kickoff, customer webinars

Timeline: 8-12 weeks preparation

‚ö° Tier 2: Significant Feature

Impact: Improves core value prop, drives upsells, expands use cases.

Examples: Advanced analytics, integrations, workflow automation

GTM Requirements: Product, Marketing, CS alignment. Feature marketing, docs, in-app announcements, blog post

Timeline: 4-6 weeks preparation

üíö Tier 3: Minor Improvement

Impact: Quality of life improvements, bug fixes, incremental enhancements.

Examples: UI polish, performance improvements, small UX fixes

GTM Requirements: Release notes, support docs, changelog entry. Minimal cross-functional coordination

Timeline: 1-2 weeks preparation

üìä Key GTM Readiness Metrics

Track these metrics to measure launch readiness and identify risks:

üìà % Completion by Workstream

What it is: % of deliverables completed for each of the 6 workstreams

Target: 100% across all workstreams before launch (or 95%+ with documented exceptions)

Why it matters: Identifies which teams need support. If Marketing is at 60% completion 3 days before launch, that's a red flag requiring intervention.

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Score (Likelihood √ó Impact)

What it is: Quantified risk based on probability (1-5) √ó impact (1-5) = score up to 25

Categories: Low (1-5), Medium (6-12), High (13-18), Critical (19-25)

Why it matters: Forces teams to surface and quantify risks early. High-risk items get mitigation plans before they become blockers.

üöß Open Blocker Count

What it is: Number of P0 issues that must be resolved before launch

Target: 0 blockers by T-3 days. Any blockers closer to launch trigger delay decisions.

Why it matters: Blockers are binary‚Äîthey prevent launch. Tracking count and burn-down rate shows if team will hit launch date.

üîó Dependency Resolution %

What it is: % of cross-team dependencies that have been resolved or have clear owners

Example: Marketing needs pricing from Product, Sales needs demo from Eng, CS needs docs from Product

Why it matters: Unresolved dependencies cause last-minute scrambles. Map dependencies early and assign DRIs (Directly Responsible Individuals).

üìÖ Days to Launch (Countdown)

What it is: Business days remaining until go-live date

Use: Creates urgency and helps teams prioritize. "We're T-5 days" focuses attention.

Why it matters: Time pressure accelerates decisions. Track burn-down of tasks vs days remaining to identify if you're on pace.

üéØ Overall Launch Readiness Score

What it is: Weighted average of all workstream completions + inverse of risk score

Formula: (Avg % Complete √ó 0.7) + ((25 - Avg Risk) / 25 √ó 0.3) √ó 100

Why it matters: Single number for exec teams: "We're at 82% readiness." Below 80% = consider delay. 90%+ = green light.
